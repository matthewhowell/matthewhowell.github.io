A New, Old Website

Published on February 05, 2023

Today, there is a new matthewhowell.net, something like the seventh version (though, I've been a poor steward of my own personal website archives).

A New Generator

The primary reason for the relaunch the site is to switch from Hugo (a very popular SSG) to Zola (a less popular SSG). I used Hugo for more than 4 years, but never really felt comfortable with. It's quite powerful, but not always straightforward, particularly its incredibly complex templating language.

It's also worth mentioning that 11ty is quite nice and Zack Leatherman is doing some wonderful work for the Web. For me, for a personal site, 11ty is just not the right fit (because Javascript). It's difficult to quantify the enormous quality of life improvement that I get from avoiding the entire NPM/Node.js/Javascript ecosystem at the moment.

So, Zola it is.

New Content

The new site comes with a bit of new content. I've added /photos, redesigned the /reading, included a (work-in-progress) public style guide. The old /projects section has become /work.

Goals of a personal website, your mileage may vary. As someone who has and hopes to continue to make things for the web, what am I asking of my own space here?

  • Be a hub of writing, work, information, and experimentation
  • Be a feed of activity
  • Be a single point of information on what it is I'm up to right now (working on)
  • Be a place for work to live: professional and personal projects
  • Be in itself an application of my own current opinions about building websites
  • Be a personal archive, a bookshelf
  • Be, to some degree, a reflection of my own design philosophy and reflect a personal style and uniqueness. It should feel like me.

The compass, notably, was not invented a tool for navigation, but for divining

Last summer, my wife and I traded our two-bedroom apartment on the second floor of a brick row home in Queens for a two-bedroom apartment on the 40th floor of a steel tower in Bangkok.

That move, entirely across the planet and tens of meters up into the air, has left us recalibrating. Our lives, in some very broad ways, are different than they were. And so, we adjust. We tinker with our routines and our behaviors.

And that's part of the great promise of a relocation, right? A clean slate. A fresh start. A spotless bathroom. A new set of neighbors. A new version of ourselves,

fitted to our new environment.

There are a few broad changes, the obvious things that you can plan around: a new job, a new apartment, . But, there are many more tiny ones. Thousands of those atomic specifics that, like a PEN METAPHOR, sktech together our days.[^]

[^]: A few examples: the pitch of a stretch of sidewalk, the thickness of the air in afternoon shade, the sound an elevator emits to announce its arrival, the force required to turn a key in our front doorand the sounds of tumblers falling into place, the color of our curtains, the depth of a couch. The number and location of motorcycles. The temperature of a glass of water. The speed of an escalator. There are thousands more.

Along with all of these adjustments in our actual, meatspace lives, the move has prompted me to rethink my home on the Web, which may certainly sound silly. It has me reconsidering what exactly this website is and what it means to be. Remember when we used to call websites homepages?

For me, and maybe for some others, these little Web houses can feel substantial. We spend years in them. Building, designing, tinkering, rebuilding, redesigning, neglecting and then sometimes rediscovering them. Futzing around in public, we share pieces of ourselves.

I have maintained some kind of personal website, on and off, since the late 1990s. Admittedly, it's been more often off.

And so we get here, to my personal site, this dusty little shelf in some far off corner of the Web.

A place of my own making, but that exists for anyone who may stumble upon it. Including, my slightly future self.

So, what responsibilities might this site carry for those groups of peoples.

  • myself, today and in the future
  • my family, friends, and colleagues
  • anyone else

What is the web?

The Web is something like a magical public library. One with unlimited shelf space and unlimited copies of everything on those shelves. a universal and immediate retrieval system, 3.2 billion card holders, each of whom are welcome to store their own stuff on a library shelf.

Privacy

Though this website primary exists for its author, it is a bit more than just my home on the web.

When you punch this URL into your web browse'n whatsit, some really wonderful stuff happens, and in the end, your browser gets a shiny copy of this website delivered to it.

So what values do I hold? I have deep, unsettling concerns about privacy and our collective loss of it. The Internet is something of minefield.

And so my own position on that grid, as a person who makes software for a living, is often conflicted.
